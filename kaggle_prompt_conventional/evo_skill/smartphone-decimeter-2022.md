### Data Processing
- **Observation/Action:** Aggregates high-frequency, irregular raw GNSS and IMU streams into fixed 1-second epochs using mean/max/first operations. Converts the Weighted Least Squares (WLS) ECEF coordinates to base latitude/longitude, then computes target deltas (`delta_lat`, `delta_lon`) relative to ground truth. Missing epoch aggregates are zero-filled.
- **ML Rationale (Why it works):** Raw smartphone sensors operate at 10–100Hz with jitter and packet loss. Aggregating to 1Hz aligns the input frequency with the evaluation cadence and leverages the law of large numbers to suppress high-frequency sensor noise. Converting to ECEF first avoids the mathematical singularities and non-linear distortions of lat/lon near poles or date lines, while predicting *deltas* transforms a non-stationary, large-magnitude regression into a stationary, low-variance residual learning problem. Zero-filling maintains tensor continuity for sequence models without introducing NaN propagation.
- **Guiding Principle:** When fusing asynchronous, high-frequency sensor streams for lower-frequency targets, resample to the target temporal resolution using domain-appropriate aggregators (mean for noise reduction, max for signal peaks). Always frame geospatial or kinematic prediction as learning a *correction* to a deterministic baseline rather than predicting absolute coordinates, and ensure temporal continuity through safe imputation before feeding sequence models.

### Feature Engineering
- **Observation/Action:** Extracts minimal but high-signal features: mean/max `Cn0DbHz` (carrier-to-noise ratio), satellite count, mean WLS ECEF coordinates, and mean accelerometer/gyroscope/magnetometer axes. These are directly standardized and fed into the model.
- **ML Rationale (Why it works):** `Cn0DbHz` and satellite count act as direct proxies for measurement quality and Geometric Dilution of Precision (GDOP). Low CN0 or few satellites indicate multipath or urban canyon conditions where GNSS errors spike. IMU readings provide short-term kinematic constraints that bridge GNSS outages and capture device orientation changes. By feeding the WLS baseline alongside these quality indicators, the model learns to *weight* or *correct* the baseline dynamically based on real-time signal reliability, effectively mimicking an adaptive Kalman filter without explicit state-space modeling.
- **Guiding Principle:** In sensor fusion, explicitly engineer features that quantify signal uncertainty or environmental difficulty (SNR, satellite geometry, fix status). Use a strong physics-based or algorithmic baseline as the primary input feature, and let the ML model learn the conditional correction weights based on real-time quality proxies rather than attempting to reconstruct absolute states from raw noise.

### Model Architecture
- **Observation/Action:** Employs a Bidirectional LSTM (2 layers, hidden dim=128) operating on a sliding window of 20 seconds. The model outputs predictions only for the final timestep of each window.
- **ML Rationale (Why it works):** GNSS positioning errors exhibit strong temporal autocorrelation due to persistent multipath reflections, slow satellite geometry shifts, and device inertia. A BiLSTM captures both historical context (for smoothing past noise) and future context (for post-processing refinement, which the competition explicitly permits). Predicting only the last timestep in a sliding window implements a learned smoothing filter: the model sees the full 20s context but outputs a single corrected state, preventing error accumulation while maximizing temporal information utilization. The 20s window balances capturing dominant error cycles (e.g., satellite passes) with maintaining local stationarity and computational tractability.
- **Guiding Principle:** For temporal error-correction tasks where post-processing is allowed, use sequence models with sliding windows that predict the target at the window boundary. This architecture naturally implements a data-driven smoother. Keep window lengths short enough to preserve local error stationarity and avoid vanishing gradients, but long enough to encompass the dominant physical or environmental cycles driving the noise.

### Validation & Training Strategy
- **Observation/Action:** Uses `GroupKFold` split by `drive` to prevent route leakage. Optimizes MSE loss on delta coordinates while tracking the competition metric (mean of 50th/95th percentile Haversine errors) during validation. Implements gradient accumulation, AMP, and explicit generalization gap monitoring.
- **ML Rationale (Why it works):** Grouping by drive ensures the model never sees segments of the same route in both train and validation splits, forcing it to learn generalizable GNSS error patterns rather than memorizing specific road geometries or urban canyon layouts. MSE on deltas strongly correlates with Euclidean distance minimization, which aligns well with the Haversine-based percentile metric. Explicit gap auditing catches overfitting to specific environmental conditions early, while gradient accumulation enables stable optimization on limited VRAM without sacrificing effective batch size.
- **Guiding Principle:** Always group validation splits by the highest-level independent experimental unit (e.g., drive, subject, device, or location) to eliminate spatial/temporal leakage. When the evaluation metric is non-differentiable or percentile-based, optimize a smooth proxy loss (MSE/Huber on relative coordinates) that mathematically correlates with the target metric. Rigorously monitor train/val generalization gaps to ensure the model learns robust physical relationships rather than dataset-specific artifacts.
