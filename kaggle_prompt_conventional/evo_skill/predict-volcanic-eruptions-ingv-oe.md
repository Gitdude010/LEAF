### Data Processing
- **Observation/Action:** Per-segment z-score normalization, hard clipping to `[-3.0, 3.0]`, NaN imputation with `0.0`, STFT transformation with log-magnitude compression, and full pre-loading of tensors into RAM.
- **ML Rationale (Why it works):** Seismic signals are inherently non-stationary, with varying baseline drifts, sensor gains, and noise floors across different 10-minute segments. Segment-wise normalization removes these distributional shifts, forcing the model to learn relative waveform patterns rather than absolute amplitudes. Hard clipping mitigates the influence of extreme volcanic spikes that would otherwise dominate gradients and destabilize optimization. The STFT projects non-stationary time-series into a quasi-stationary time-frequency domain where eruption precursors manifest as localized energy shifts across specific frequency bands. The `log1p` transform compresses the dynamic range, approximating a more Gaussian distribution and preventing gradient explosion during backpropagation. RAM caching decouples slow disk I/O from GPU compute, enabling deterministic training loops and higher epoch throughput.
- **Guiding Principle:** For raw sensor/time-series data, always apply instance/segment-wise normalization to handle non-stationarity, use robust clipping for heavy-tailed outliers, and project data into a domain where the target phenomenon is spectrally compact. Cache preprocessed tensors to eliminate I/O bottlenecks and guarantee reproducibility across runs.

### Feature Engineering
- **Observation/Action:** STFT parameters (`n_fft=256, hop_length=64`) generate ~129 frequency bins per sensor. The 10 spatial sensors and frequency bins are flattened into a single channel dimension (`1290`) before feeding into a 1D CNN.
- **ML Rationale (Why it works):** Volcanic tremors propagate through heterogeneous crustal layers and are recorded with phase/amplitude differences across spatially distributed sensors. Flattening spatial (sensor) and spectral (frequency) dimensions into the channel axis allows 1D convolutions to learn joint spatio-spectral correlations through shared temporal kernels. This design treats the multi-channel spectrogram as a unified "signal," enabling the network to detect coherent frequency-band energy shifts that precede eruptions, regardless of which specific sensor captures them first or how the energy is distributed across the array. It effectively replaces expensive 2D convolutions or cross-attention with a parameter-efficient, translation-equivariant representation.
- **Guiding Principle:** When modeling multi-channel time-series, consider flattening modalities or frequency bands into the channel dimension for 1D convolutions. This forces the architecture to learn cross-channel and cross-frequency interactions efficiently, acting as a scalable alternative to 2D CNNs or attention mechanisms for temporal prediction tasks where joint spatio-spectral evolution is predictive.

### Model Architecture
- **Observation/Action:** A hierarchical 1D CNN with progressive downsampling (`Conv1d → BatchNorm → GELU → AvgPool1d` repeated 4×), culminating in `AdaptiveAvgPool1d(1)` and a lightweight MLP regression head.
- **ML Rationale (Why it works):** The hierarchical pooling progressively expands the receptive field, allowing early layers to capture short-term tremor anomalies and deeper layers to integrate longer-term precursory trends. `AdaptiveAvgPool1d` provides temporal translation invariance, focusing the model on the *presence and evolution* of spectral patterns rather than their exact alignment within the 10-minute window. This is critical because eruption precursors may manifest at varying times relative to the segment start. The shallow MLP head prevents bottlenecking of rich hierarchical features and reduces overfitting to the regression target, which is common when the head has excessive capacity relative to the signal-to-noise ratio.
- **Guiding Principle:** Use hierarchical 1D CNNs with global pooling for time-series regression when predictive patterns are temporally misaligned or variable within the input window. Progressive receptive field expansion captures both local anomalies and global trends. Keep the final regression head lightweight to preserve the representational capacity of the feature extractor and avoid over-parameterizing the mapping to the target.

### Validation & Training Strategy
- **Observation/Action:** 3-Fold CV with fold-specific target normalization, `SmoothL1Loss`, AdamW with low LR (`8e-5`), CosineAnnealingLR, gradient accumulation (4 steps), gradient clipping (`norm=1.0`), `bfloat16` mixed precision, fold-averaged ensembling, and explicit train-val gap monitoring.
- **ML Rationale (Why it works):** Fold-specific target normalization prevents data leakage from global statistics into validation folds and centers the regression target, which stabilizes gradient magnitudes and aligns with the loss function's operating range. `SmoothL1Loss` (Huber) is robust to outliers in `time_to_eruption`, preventing gradient explosion from segments with extreme lead times while maintaining differentiability near zero. Low LR + weight decay + cosine scheduling + gradient clipping ensures stable convergence in a noisy, high-dimensional loss landscape typical of physical signal regression. Gradient accumulation approximates larger batch sizes for more reliable gradient estimates without VRAM overflow. Explicitly monitoring the train-val gap detects memorization of segment IDs or noise rather than learning physical precursors, acting as an early warning system for distributional mismatch.
- **Guiding Principle:** For regression on noisy, heavy-tailed physical data, normalize targets per fold to prevent leakage, use robust loss functions, and employ conservative optimization with explicit gradient control. Treat the generalization gap as a first-class diagnostic metric to ensure the model learns transferable physical signals rather than dataset-specific artifacts. Ensemble fold predictions to reduce variance from random initialization and fold-split bias.
