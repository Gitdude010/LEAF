2. Local CV

One of the most important objectives of this competition is to evaluate model performance on drugs that have never appeared in the public dataset.  
To this end, I used the CV stratification code posted by @cdeotte (https://www.kaggle.com/c/lish-moa/discussion/195195).

In genomics and biomedical data, factors such as sample type, instrument error, reagent quality, and other systematic deviations can significantly affect data distribution. Such effects are inevitable in real-world settings, making proper evaluation critically important. For example, when batch effects exist, “leave-batch-out” cross-validation reflects a model’s generalization ability more objectively than random k-fold splitting.  
In this competition—though not entirely certain—this “leave-drug-out” strategy appears to reduce the risk of overfitting to a specific group of drugs (public LB rank: 4th; private LB rank: 2nd).

3. Pre-processing

Sample Normalization

Some assumptions (potentially incorrect) were made:

- Raw biological data are often incomparable due to inevitable systematic errors (e.g., data cannot be generated on the same machine; experiments cannot be conducted by the same person).  
- Even under vastly different treatments, the number of significantly different features will not be excessively large.  
- It can be reasonably assumed that the distributions of most samples are similar.  

Based on these assumptions, samples were normalized separately for gene expression and cell viability.

In short, for each sample, the gene expression data were centered by subtracting the mean of the 25th and 75th percentiles.  
Similarly, the cell viability data were centered by subtracting the mean of the 25th and 72nd percentiles, then scaled by dividing by the sum of the 4th and 75th percentiles of the resulting distribution. The slightly differing steps for gene and cell data were determined by the normality of their respective distributions after transformation.

Generally, sample normalization should be performed before feature transformation.

Feature Transformation

After sample normalization, quantile transformation was applied to numerical features.

4. Feature Engineering

There is nothing particularly novel in feature engineering—not even variance filtering in the 1D-CNN model. Different feature processing methods across the other two models were employed solely to increase model diversity.

PCA features were used across all models, with varying numbers of components (50 genes + 15 cells for 1D-CNN; 600 genes + 50 cells for TabNet and DNN).

Statistical features (e.g., sum, mean) and interaction features were used in TabNet.

Variance filtering was applied in both TabNet and DNN.

Additionally, dummy variables (cp_time, cp_dose) were removed in all models.

5. Loss

To address target class imbalance and mitigate overfitting to specific targets, Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss with label smoothing and positive-sample weighting (pos_weight) was used in all individual models.

As noted by others, label smoothing improved performance by reducing overconfident probability estimates.

Furthermore, target weights were slightly adjusted using the reciprocal of class frequency.

Specifically, for each target *i*, the weight was set to log(*F*<sub>min</sub> + 100) / log(*F*<sub>i</sub> + 100), where *F*<sub>i</sub> denotes the number of positive samples for target *i*, and *F*<sub>min</sub> denotes the minimum positive count across all targets. The constant term 100 was added to prevent extremely low-frequency targets (e.g., those with only one positive sample) from disproportionately influencing the model, and the logarithmic transformation ensures smoothness.

6. Modelling

1) 1D-CNN

This individual model achieves the best performance in this approach (private score: 0.01601). Applying such an architecture to tabular data is motivated by the idea that:

- CNNs excel at feature extraction but are rarely used for tabular data because the correct ordering of features is unknown.  
- A simple idea is to reshape the data directly into a multi-channel image format, allowing the correct ordering to be learned via fully connected (FC) layers through backpropagation.  

Model Architecture  

Based on these ideas, a model that extracts features using 1D-CNN (which empirically outperformed both 2D and 3D variants) was implemented. The figure below shows its main structure.

As shown above, the feature dimension is first expanded via an FC layer. This layer serves two purposes: (i) increasing dimensionality to provide sufficient “pixels” for the image representation, and (ii) enabling meaningful image generation by learning appropriate feature ordering.  
Next, the data are reshaped directly into image format (size: 16×1, channels: 256).  
Like standard CNNs, features are extracted through several subsequent 1D convolutional layers, incorporating shortcut-like connections.  
Finally, the flattened extracted features are passed through an FC layer to predict the targets.

Pre-training  

Additionally, non-scored targets were used exclusively for pre-training. In this step, only the top 33 non-scored targets most highly correlated with the scored targets were trained—for just one epoch. The sole purpose was to achieve better model initialization. Including irrelevant targets or performing additional training epochs could lead to unintended consequences.

2) TabNet  

The TabNet implementation used here is a slight modification of a public kernel (https://www.kaggle.com/kushal1506/moa-tabnet-inference). Aside from the preprocessing method described above, there is nothing distinctive.

3) DNN  

Similarly, the DNN model is a modified version adapted from a public notebook (https://www.kaggle.com/thehemen/pytorch-transfer-learning-with-k-folds-by-drug-ids/comments), featuring pre-training on all targets (both scored and non-scored), followed by fine-tuning on the scored targets only.

7. Blending  

The three individual models are ensembled to produce the final submission. Compared with the 1D-CNN alone, the final ensemble yields only a marginal improvement of 0.00002 (private LB: 0.01599)—possibly due to insufficient model diversity or too few individual models.
